Data Structure for linear mixed effect model.¶

In this example, we have two groups (Disease vs. Control), and we measured their FIB-4 score at baseline (prior to treatment), 6 months after treatment, and 12 months after treatment. We hypothesized that the trend of FIB-4 score over time will be different between the two groups (i.e. we expect an interaction between study group and timepoints).

Here is an example of data structure for linear mixed effect model: Subject ID | Group |Time Point | Outcome ---------- | ------- |---------- | -------- 1 | 1 |1 | 10 1 | 1 |2 | 12 1 | 1 |3 | 14 2 | 0 |1 | 8 2 | 0 |2 | 10 2 | 0 |3 | 12

import pandas as pd
import random

# Set a seed for reproducibility
random.seed(108)

# Generating random data
study_ids = [f'STUDY_{i}' for i in range(1, 101)]
data = []

for study_id in study_ids:
    group = random.choice(['CONTROL', 'DISEASE'])
    age = random.randint(18, 65)
    sex = random.choice(['FEMALE', 'MALE'])
    
    for time_block in ['BASELINE', 'POST_6M', 'POST_12M']:
        fib4 = round(random.uniform(0, 3), 2)
        data.append([study_id, group, age, sex, time_block, fib4])

# Creating DataFrame
df = pd.DataFrame(data, columns=['STUDY_ID', 'GROUP', 'AGE', 'SEX', 'TIME_BLOCK', 'FIB4'])

# Displaying the DataFrame
print(df.head())

What is linear mixed effect model and when should we use it?¶

A Linear Mixed Effects Model (LMM) extends the general linear model (GLM) by incorporating both fixed effects and random effects. It is typically used when observations are not independent but can be grouped within certain clusters or levels. In our example, observations of FIB-4 score at each time point (TIME_BLOCK) are not independent but grouped at the subject level (STUDY_ID). Thus, 'STUDY_ID', 'GROUP', 'AGE', 'SEX', 'TIME_BLOCK' are our fixed effects because these parameters are assumed to be constant across all levels of the groupiong factor. 'STUDY_ID' is our random effect because each subject may have a different baseline of FIB4 score prior to the treatment (i.e. random intercept), and each subject may show different rate of change of FIB-4 score over TIME_BLOCK (i.e. random slope).

Note that linear mixed effect quantifies the relationships between a continuous dependent variable and various predictor variables. Thus, for dependent variable, the linear mixed effect models assume continuous and normally distributed residuals. I will show you how to peform mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression using FIB-4 score in ordinal category in later chapter.

Convert TIME_BLOCK in categorical to numerical so as to capture linear trend.¶

Since it is equal time interval between baseline, post-6m, and post-12m, we can treat it as numerical (0, 1, 2). This numerical format assume a constant rate of change in the outcome (FIB-4) over timepoint. Later in model specification, you can still do C(Timepoint) to treat Timepoint as categorical variable, so that post-6m (1) and post-12m (2) can be compared to reference timepoint baseline (0).

What covariates to include the linear mixed effect model?¶

Our main interest is the interaction between Group (Control vs Disease) and Timepoint (0, 1, 2), because we hypothesize having the disease (fatty liver) would change the short-term (6 months) or long-term (12 months) effectiveness of the treatment of hep C (measured by FIB-4 score). This interaction effect is formulated as GROUP*C(TIMEPOINT) in the model. The asterisk sign indicates interaction, whereas the plus sign (+) in the model formula indicates linear effects of A + B.

In addition to GROUP*C(TIMEPOINT) interaction, we also want to control for other confounding variables that may contribute to the change of FIB-4 score over time due to the treatment, such as demographic variables like sex, age, etc. It is always a good idea to graphically see what variables differ significantly between control vs. disease group.

Let's create a linear mixed effect model with a random intercept and a random slope for a given group (in our case, group=STUDY_ID).¶

First, the "group" in this example refers to the object being measured repeatedly (i.e. STUDY_ID), not the study assignment (control vs. disease). Since there might be individual differences between groups that are not explicitly modeled by the fixed effect, we can add a random intercept to capture the difference in the baseline level of the outcome variable (FIB-4 score) across groups. In addition, there might also be difference in the steepness of the slope across groups. In our example, the hep c treatment, which administered after baseline timepoint, might have different effect on the post-6-month and post-12-month FIB-4 score across groups, with some subjects showing a steep descend slope whereas others showing a shallow descend.

  • To include a random intercept for each group: re_formula="1". However, since this is the default, you don't need to explicitly specify it in the model.
  • To include a random slope without a random intercept: re_formula="0+C(TIMEPOINT)"
  • To include both a random intercept and a random slope while assuming dependence between the two: re_formula="1 + C(TIMEPOINT)" --> this dependence/correlation indicates that the subject with higher FIB4 at baseline tend to have steeper decline of FIB4 score over time (after treatment).
  • To include both a random intercept and a random slope while assuming independence between the two: vc_formula = {'TIMEPOINT' : '0 + C(TIMEPOINT)'}

In this linear mixed effect model, we allow both the intercept and the slope to change for each group. We also assume interactions between the intercepts and the slopes.¶

import statsmodels.api as sm
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# Construct the formula with the interaction term. Here I used C(TIMEPOINT) for categorical Timepoint.
formula = "FIB4 ~ GROUP * C(TIMEPOINT) + AGE + C(SEX, Treatment(reference='FEMALE'))"

# fitting the mixed effect model (here we include a random slope and a randon intercept per group)
mixedlm_model = smf.mixedlm(formula, data=df, groups=df['STUDY_ID'], missing='drop', re_formula="1+C(TIMEPOINT)").fit()

Let's go through each variable in the model and interpret its coefficients:¶

  1. Intercept (1.546):

    • This is the estimated intercept when all other predictor variables are set to zero. In this case, it represents the estimated value of the dependent variable when the categorical variables are at their reference levels, and the continuous variables are at zero. Since our reference study group is control group, the intercept here means the baseline FIB-4 score is 1.546 for control group at the baseline timpeoint (TIMEPOINT=0).

  2. GROUP[T.DISEASE] (0.011):

    • This represents the effect of being in the disease group compared to the control group when all other variables are held constant. The coefficient is 0.011, suggesting the baseline FIB-4 score for disease group is 1.557 (1.546+0.011).

  3. C(TIMEPOINT)[T.1] (0.149):

    • This represents the effect of being at the first timepoint compared to the baseline (reference level) when all other variables are held constant. The coefficient is 0.149, suggesting the baseline FIB-4 score for T.1 is 1.695 (1.546+0.149).

  4. C(TIMEPOINT)[T.2] (-0.002):

    • This represents the effect of being at the second timepoint compared to the baseline (reference level) when all other variables are held constant. The coefficient is -0.002, suggesting the baseline FIB-4 score for T.1 is 1.544 (1.546-0.002).

  5. C(SEX, Treatment(reference='FEMALE'))[T.MALE] (0.078):

    • This represents the effect of being male (compared to the reference level 'FEMALE') when all other variables are held constant. The coefficient is 0.078, suggesting the FIB-4 score for males at the baseline timepoint is approximately 1.624(1.546+0.078).

  6. GROUP[T.DISEASE]:C(TIMEPOINT)[T.1] (-0.065):

    • This represents the interaction effect between the disease group and being at the first timepoint compared to the control group at the baseline. The coefficient is -0.065, suggesting the FIB-4 score for disease control at T.1 minus baseline is 0.065 less than that of control group. From above, you can actually calculate 1) Control at T0 = 1.546, (2) Control at T1 =1.695, (3) Disease at T0 =1.557 and (4) Disease at T1 = 1.641 (1.546+0.011+0.149−0.065)

  7. GROUP[T.DISEASE]:C(TIMEPOINT)[T.2] (-0.218):

    • This represents the interaction effect between the disease group and being at the second timepoint compared to the control group at the baseline. The coefficient is -0.218, suggesting the FIB-4 score for disease control at T.2 minus baseline is 0.218 less than that of control group.

  8. AGE (-0.003):

    • This represents the effect of a one-unit increase in age on the dependent variable when all other variables are held constant. The coefficient is -0.003, suggesting one unit increase in age will result in a decrease of 0.003 in FIB-4.

  9. TIMEPOINT Var (0.370):

    • This represents the variance of the random effect for the TIMEPOINT variable. It indicates the variability in the effect of TIMEPOINT across different groups.

odds_ratios = {}

# Loop through the model results and calculate odds ratios
for param, coef in mixedlm_model.params.items():
    # Skip the intercept terms
    if 'Intercept' in param:
        continue

    # Calculate odds ratio
    odds_ratio = np.exp(coef)

    # Store the odds ratio in the dictionary
    odds_ratios[param] = odds_ratio

# Print the results
for param, odds_ratio in odds_ratios.items():
    print(f"Odds Ratio for {param}: {odds_ratio:.3f}")
